The Crack-Spread Prison: Why Conventional Refineries Must Hedge

For over a century, oil refineries have been prisoners of the crack spread - the volatile difference between crude oil costs and refined product prices. This fundamental vulnerability forces conventional refineries into a multi-billion dollar hedging trap that consumes a lot of capital and management attention while generating high-risk and negative returns.

The Restaurant Analogy

Imagine running a restaurant where your main ingredient cost can double overnight, but customers refuse to pay proportionally higher prices. When crude oil prices spike, fuel consumers behave exactly like these restaurant customers - they dramatically reduce consumption rather than accept higher prices.

Consumer Demand Sensitivity: The Core Problem

When crude oil prices rise, conventional refineries become trapped between two devastating choices: raise prices and watch customer volumes plummet from reduced driving, or hold prices steady and watch profit margins squeezed. In the last 10 years, 16 U.S. refineries closed - cutting over 40-million gallons a day of fuel supplies amidst growing consumer demand. With reduced refinery production supplies, consumers pay higher retail fuel prices.
